How to document your art career: certificates, exhibition records, and archives

July 1, 2026Editorial team7 min read
Cover image — replace

An exhibition that cannot be verified might as well not have happened. For artists building a professional record — for galleries, residencies, academic positions, or institutional review — the difference between activity and documentation is decisive.

What counts as documentation

A useful record has three properties: it names you unambiguously, it names the evaluating body and its credentials, and it can be checked by a third party without your involvement. A numbered certificate that a reviewer can verify online meets all three; a screenshot of a social media post meets none.

Juried results carry more weight than open publication because they document a selection decision made by identifiable professionals. This is why the credentials of the jury matter as much as the result itself — a record is only as strong as the body that issued it.

Keep every certificate number. A record you cannot cite precisely is a record you do not have.

Building the archive

Maintain a single chronological list: competition or exhibition name, organizing institution, date, result, certificate or reference number, and a link to the public archive entry. Update it the day a result is published, not months later. Where a platform maintains a permanent winners archive, link to your entry rather than to a homepage.

Publications belong in the same list. A peer-reviewed article with a DOI, an interview published by a curatorial platform, a catalogue entry — each is a dated, checkable fact. The archive as a whole should read the way an institution reads: dry, precise, and verifiable.
